# A Guide to Yatay Geçiş for YTB Scholarship Students

If you are not satisfied with your current placement, you are not alone — and you do have options. This guide walks through the steps involved in transferring universities as a YTB scholarship student, based on a transfer that is still in progress. Because it is being written alongside a live application, it will be expanded and refined over time.

:::warning Set your expectations early
The probability of a successful transfer is low. This is not meant to discourage you, but to keep your expectations realistic. Universities are generally reluctant to take on the administrative work of admitting a scholarship student — who brings them no tuition — into an already established cohort. As you go through this process, it is worth also appreciating the place you are currently in; it almost certainly has its own strengths.
:::

Throughout the guide, personal experience is used as the running example. Your own process may differ in the details, so treat these accounts as a reference point rather than a fixed rulebook. (Medicine programs, in particular, are reported to follow different transfer mechanics that are not covered here.)

## What is yatay geçiş?

:::note In short
The transfer you are looking for is **kurumlar arası yatay geçiş**. Use that exact phrase when searching for information.
:::

*Yatay geçiş* translates literally to "horizontal transfer." Türkiye recognizes two categories of transfer: **yatay** (horizontal) and **dikey** (vertical). Vertical transfer applies when you want to change your field of study entirely. Field and university placement in Türkiye is largely determined by the national high-school exit examination, the ÖSYS. As an international student, you most likely have not sat this exam, as it is administered to local students only.

Horizontal transfer keeps you in the same field while moving you between universities — for example, remaining in Computer Engineering (*bilgisayar mühendisliği*) at a different institution. Within yatay geçiş there are several sub-types:

| Type | What it covers | Relevant to you? |
|---|---|---|
| **Kurum içi yatay geçiş** | Transfer between different fields within the *same* university | No |
| **Kurumlar arası yatay geçiş** | Transfer between two *different* universities in the *same* field | **Yes** |

Kurumlar arası yatay geçiş is itself divided into two forms. The distinction matters mainly when you review a university's quota (*kontenjan*), discussed later:

- **Yurt içi** — transfer from a university located inside Türkiye. *This is the one that applies to you.*
- **Yurt dışı** — transfer from a university located outside Türkiye.

So the precise term to search for is **kurumlar arası [yurt içi] yatay geçiş**.

:::important Transfer student vs. first-year student
There is one distinction you must guard against. Universities want students, and they will often present a convenient-sounding option: *"In case the transfer does not work out, you can enroll as a first-year student and we will transfer your existing course credits."*

On the surface this seems harmless — your credits are not wasted. For a YTB scholarship holder, however, it is a serious problem: enrolling as a first-year student effectively extends the duration of your studies, and therefore the cost your scholarship must cover.

**Make sure you apply for, and are admitted into, the second or third year specifically — not as a first-year student whose credits were transferred.**
:::

## When should you start preparing?

:::note In short
The active work begins around mid-to-late June, but your GPA matters from day one — so treat every semester as part of your transfer preparation.
:::

Application timelines here differ significantly from those you may be used to. Many universities elsewhere accept transfer or first-year applications between September and April. In Türkiye, transfer applications open **after** the academic year has concluded — typically late June, throughout July, and into early August.

That said, preparation begins long before the application window. As a university student, your strongest asset is your academic record, and most institutions explicitly require that you have **passed all of your courses** to be considered for transfer. Your results carry substantial weight, so start building a strong record early.

In summary: the practical transfer work begins at the end of the academic year — around late June and early July — with the bulk of the effort falling across July and August.

## What do you actually need?

:::note In short
Every university sets its own requirements. Find that university's yatay geçiş **yönergesi** (directive) to confirm exactly what it asks for. The most common requirements are listed below.
:::

Comparisons ultimately come down to numbers: scores, grades, competition results, and academic standing. Which numbers matter is where it becomes complicated. Some universities require ÖSYS scores; others are more flexible with international applicants. This raises the central point of this section — how to obtain reliable information.

:::tip Always go to the source
By government requirement, every university must publish a **yönergesi** (directive) setting out its horizontal and vertical transfer rules. This directive is your only fully verified source of information for that institution's process. Some universities bury it deep within their website and it can take considerable time to locate, but it is worth the effort. Read the directive carefully — or have a tool such as ChatGPT help you read it.
:::

The most common requirements are as follows.

### 1. ÖSYS results

As an international student, you cannot take the ÖSYS — it is simply not available to you, so there is no need to worry about obtaining it.

The ÖSYS exists for university placement in Türkiye, so a suitable substitute is the examination that earned you your scholarship in the first place. Commonly accepted alternatives include:

- **SAT** — broadly accepted as a stand-in for ÖSYS results.
- **ACT** — less widely accepted than the SAT, but still useful where recognized.
- **Your national exam**, if you have one — the high-school exit examination from your home country. This is a strong option because it is a national exam, though many countries are not formally recognized by many Turkish universities (understandably, given there are more than 180 countries to track).
- **Your high-school transcript** — also a reasonable option.

:::warning Confirm before submitting
These are examples of documents you may be able to submit in place of the ÖSYS. Always confirm with the specific university before submitting anything.
:::

Substituting the exam does not fully resolve the university's underlying problem: how to compare you quantitatively against local students whose metrics differ from yours. The common solution is to use the **base entrance score** (*taban puanı*) for your field in your year of entry. Each year, the lowest ÖSYS score admitted into each field is recorded and published as that field's *taban puanı*. Because you do not have an ÖSYS score, universities will often default to this base score when assessing your merit. Some universities ask you to supply the official base score for your field and entry year yourself, rather than looking it up. You can find this at **yokatlas.yok.gov.tr** (see the resources section at the end). As always, confirm whether this applies by checking the directive or contacting the university directly.

### 2. ÖSYS placement document

:::note Accuracy not yet confirmed
The information in this section is not fully verified — treat it with caution.
:::

This requirement is an extension of the previous one. For YTB students, the placement document is effectively your acceptance letter together with your **bursluluk belgesi** (scholarship certificate), since it records the placement you were assigned. If you find a better-suited document for this purpose, feel free to use it.

### 3. Transcript

Universities require a verified transcript and typically accept it in one of two forms:

- **From e-Devlet** (recommended, and usually the simplest). If it has not yet been updated with all of your grades, contact your university's student affairs office (*öğrenci işleri*).
- **A signed and stamped printout** issued by your university.

:::tip Mind the date
Make sure the transcript is dated no more than two weeks before your transfer application.
:::

### 4. Öğrenci belgesi (student certificate)

As with the transcript, obtain this either from e-Devlet or as a signed and stamped printout from your university.

### 5. Disiplin belgesi (disciplinary record)

You can usually download this from your university's student portal. If a technical issue prevents this — as happened in the example case — visit your student affairs office and specifically request a *disiplin belgesi* for a yatay geçiş application. In one instance this took the form of an *öğrenci belgesi* with a note confirming the student had no disciplinary record and no outstanding penalty fees.

### 6. Kimlik (ID)

Scan and upload.

### 7. Passport

Scan and upload.

### 8. Language proficiency document

If you are transferring into an **English-language program**, you must demonstrate English proficiency. Accepted exams vary by university, but generally you must submit a result above the university's set threshold, from an exam taken within the past two years. The exams most consistently accepted are:

- **TOEFL**
- **PTE Academic**

Notably, many universities do **not** accept IELTS, Duolingo, or SAT as substitutes for proof of English proficiency. Most institutions also offer their own proficiency exam that you can take.

:::note Transferring into a Turkish-language program
The same principle applies: you must demonstrate Turkish-language proficiency. As a YTB scholar, you will already have completed a year of TÖMER through your original university, so this should not be an obstacle.
:::

These are the most commonly requested documents. Some universities require more and others fewer, so always verify the exact requirements for each institution.
